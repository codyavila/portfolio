## 1. If It Ain't Squishy, It Ain't Right

In the real world, things don't just slide linearly from A to B. If you throw a ball, it bounces. If you poke a marshmallow, it squishes.

So why do our buttons feel like concrete blocks?

I decided that every interaction needed to feel physical. I replaced standard CSS transitions with **spring physics**. When you click a button here, it doesn't just change colorâ€”it compresses. It fights back.

<Steps>
  <Step title="The Press">

  </Step>
  <Step title="The Release">
    It snaps back, overshooting slightly before settling.
  </Step>
  <Step title="The Result">
    Dopamine. Pure, unadulterated dopamine.
  </Step>
</Steps>

## 2. Glass That Actually Glows

"Glassmorphism" had a moment a few years ago, then everyone realized it was a performance nightmare and stopped.

I didn't stop. I doubled down.

But I didn't want just "frosted glass." I wanted **bioluminescent glass**. I wanted cards that felt like they were lit from within, reacting to your mouse like it was a light source.

## 3. The Sound of Silence (is Boring)

I know, I know. "Websites shouldn't have sound."

Counterpoint: **Video games have sound.** Apps have sound. Your toaster has sound. Why is the web the only silent medium?

The trick is subtlety. No autoplaying techno beats. Just tiny, satisfying *clicks*, *thuds*, and *whooshes* that confirm your actions. It adds a tactile dimension that visuals alone can't achieve.

## 4. Painting with Math

See that background? The one that looks like moving smoke or a lava lamp?

That's not a video. It's not a GIF. It's a **Fragment Shader**.

It's a tiny program running on your graphics card, calculating the color of every single pixel in real-time, 60 times a second. It's math masquerading as art. And it is surprisingly easy to get started with.
